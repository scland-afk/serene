Our Retaining Wall Installation Process

A stable wall starts with proper excavation and a compacted granular base. We install drainage systems behind the wall to manage water pressure, because hydrostatic pressure is what causes walls to bow and fail. Blocks are leveled, pinned, and backfilled in lifts with free-draining material—not clay or topsoil.

  • Site Assessment + Engineering: We measure grade change, evaluate soil conditions, and determine if permits are required for wall height.
  • Base Preparation: We excavate to proper depth, install compacted granular base, and ensure the first course sits level and below grade.
  • Drainage Installation: Perforated drain tile runs behind the wall, wrapped in filter fabric, and outlets to daylight or stormwater systems.
  • Block Installation: Each course is leveled, pinned with fiberglass or metal pins, and backfilled with clear stone in 8-inch lifts.
  • Cap Installation + Grading: Capstones are glued in place, and final grading directs water away from the wall face.

Retaining Wall Materials and Longevity

We primarily install Allan Block and similar segmental retaining wall systems designed for freeze-thaw performance. These interlock without mortar, allow controlled movement, and can be repaired or adjusted if needed. Natural stone walls are available for specific design requirements but require different installation methods.

Properly installed segmental walls last 30+ years. Failures typically result from inadequate drainage, poor base compaction, or insufficient embedment depth—not block failure.

Long-Term Performance Through Proper Installation

Results depend on engineering, not just appearance. We focus on correct base depth (minimum 6 inches of compacted ¾" clear stone), drainage that outlets properly, and backfill that allows water to move through—not around—the wall. Clean grading at the top prevents surface water from running behind the wall and creating pressure.
